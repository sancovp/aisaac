The Preliminaries

Four Thoughts That
Turn The Mind

Yogi A-La, Acharya · 14 min read · Foundations
These are called "preliminary practices" — ngöndro. People hear that word and think it means beginner stuff, like you graduate out of it. No. These are the four contemplations that, when they actually land, make the entire path feel like the only sensible response to being alive.

Before we talk about what to contemplate —
let's talk about what contemplation actually means.

Most people read these four thoughts, nod, think "yes that's true," and move on. That is not contemplation. That is information processing. Contemplation is when you take one of these premises and you sit with it — actually sit with it, not as a concept but as a lived reality — until something shifts. Until your behavior changes. Until your priorities reorganize themselves without you having to force them to.

The Tibetan tradition is very precise about this. It doesn't say "contemplate these four things and feel bad about your life." It says: penetrate these four things until the mind naturally turns toward dharma. The turning is the point. Dharma means the path, the teachings, the reality of things. When your mind turns toward it — not because someone told you to, but because you've seen clearly — that's when practice begins.

What does penetration look like? The text says there are signs. Not guarantees — signs. Indications that the contemplation is working. I'll give those to you for each one, because without them you're guessing.

"Impermanence is pervasive so Death can come at any moment and this uncertainty of the time and method of Death is its only certainty."

— Future Buddha With Your Name, Yogi A-La

The Four Thoughts

First Thought

The Preciousness of Human Birth

You were born human. You have a functioning mind, access to teachings, some measure of leisure, and the basic capacity for ethical reasoning. The tradition lists eighteen conditions — eight freedoms and ten endowments — that make a human birth what it is. Every single one of them had to be present simultaneously. None of them is guaranteed in your next life or your next moment.

The contemplation is not "I should be grateful." That's greeting-card spirituality. The contemplation is: given what I actually have right now — this mind, this circumstance, this window — what is the most reasonable use of it? Not the most enjoyable use, or the most impressive use, or the use that will make other people think well of me. The most reasonable use, given what this life actually is.

Signs it's penetrating: You find yourself in obeisance to the Three Jewels — not as performance, not as ritual for its own sake, but as a genuine recognition that the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha are the most reliable orientation available to you. The sign is that it feels obvious, not obligatory.
Second Thought

Impermanence

Everything that exists right now will not exist — including you, in your current form, with your current consciousness, in your current circumstances. This is not depressing. It is liberating, when it's actually understood. The problem is that most people hold impermanence as an intellectual concept while living as if they have unlimited time.

The specific contemplation here is death. Not as a memento mori aesthetic. As a functional reality: you do not know when it comes. You do not know how it comes. These are the only certainties — that it will come, and that you do not know when or how. That combination, truly held, destroys the logic of postponement. Everything you keep meaning to do with your life you keep postponing. The contemplation asks: what is the rational response to not knowing whether you have ten more years or ten more minutes?

Signs it's penetrating: You stop wasting time. Not in a frantic, anxious way — in a clarifying way. The mind stops parting from bodhichitta, the intention toward awakening, because it's become obvious that postponing the path is postponing the only thing that actually matters. You notice urgency without panic.
Third Thought

Karma

Cause and effect are unfailing. Every action — physical, verbal, or mental — plants a seed. Those seeds ripen. They don't ask your permission. They don't wait until it's convenient. The suffering you experience is not random and it is not unfair. It is the ripening of causes you planted, most of them without awareness, many of them from a very long time ago. And everything you plant now will ripen, whether you remember planting it or not.

This is not fatalism. Fatalism says there's nothing to be done. Karma says there is everything to be done — and that what you do now matters more than you can currently calculate. The tragedy of most human lives is that people toil in samsara, following habit, following desire, following aversion, without ever stopping to ask whether the seeds they're planting are the seeds they want to ripen. They wander in self-torture, endlessly. So does everyone counting on them.

Signs it's penetrating: You start naturally resolving experiences into the relative wisdom of what is actually skillful and beneficial. Not as rule-following. As intelligence — the intelligence of someone who understands that every action has consequences you cannot fully see, and who begins to act accordingly. Ethical sensitivity arises naturally, not as obligation.
Fourth Thought

The Pervasiveness of Samsara

Samsara means cyclical existence — the wheel of conditioned experience driven by ignorance, desire, and aversion. The contemplation here is not that samsara is unpleasant. The contemplation is that it is everywhere. There is no corner of conditioned existence where samsara doesn't reach. No relationship, no achievement, no pleasure, no state of mind that exists within conditioned experience is free from the capacity to become a source of suffering. Not because things are bad, but because the engine that generates them — ignorance of the nature of mind — keeps running.

If you don't turn your mind, you will always encounter samsara. Your transmigration — through states, through circumstances, through lives if you accept that framing — is certain. Not as punishment. As consequence of the engine that's been running since beginningless time, unexamined. The question the fourth thought poses is simple: is there a different mode? And if there is, why haven't you looked for it with everything you have?

Signs it's penetrating: The cause of gross and subtle suffering becomes apparent — not as philosophy, but as direct recognition. You see original ignorance operating in real time, in your own mind. And simultaneously — this is the pivot — you begin to recognize that this original ignorance is co-emergent with original pristine awareness. The two arise together. That means the door is already there. You're already standing in front of it. The sign is that samsara stops being something that happens to you and becomes something you can see happening, which is entirely different.

Why these four, in this order

The sequence matters. First you recognize the value of what you have. Then you recognize it won't last. Then you understand that what you do with it has consequences that extend beyond what you can see. Then you understand that the entire wheel of consequences exists within a system — samsara — that keeps generating suffering until you look at its root.

These four together form a single argument. The argument is: you have a rare and precious window, it is closing, everything you do in it has real consequences, and the system you're operating in by default will not stop generating suffering unless you take it apart at the root. That's not pessimism. That's an engineering problem. The path is the solution.

The tradition calls these preliminaries. I want to be direct with you about what that means: preliminary does not mean easy, does not mean fast, and does not mean you do them once and move on. Some people spend years on these four contemplations. Some people return to them at every stage of the path because the penetration deepens. A monk who has practiced for forty years will contemplate impermanence differently than a beginner — not because the concept changed, but because the capacity to hold it has changed.

From the text

On the donut and the hole

Here is the underlying logic, stated precisely: the a priori possibility of samsara is simply the absence of directly knowing the other a priori possibility. Each one prevents you from experiencing the other but has a path for doing so. This is a donut. The entire description of the donut includes the hole. The donut hole is dual with the donut. From the point of view of the experiencer inside the donut, they experience a constant moving forward while standing in place. Or they discover the inside wall of the donut hole. The four thoughts are how you find the wall.

You are not being asked to feel guilty about your life. You are not being asked to adopt a grim worldview or become a pessimist. You are being asked to look clearly at the situation you're actually in — the situation every human being is in — and respond to it with the full intelligence you actually have. These four thoughts are what makes that response possible. Without them, practice is just exercise. With them, it's a path.

Continue the Path

Now that the mind has turned —

The Four Noble Truths are the first teaching the Buddha gave after his awakening. Not because they're beginner material. Because they're the most precise map of where you are and where the door is. Including a donut metaphor that will rearrange how you think about duality.
