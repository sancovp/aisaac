First Turning of the Wheel

The Four Noble Truths
(The Donut)

Yogi A-La, Acharya · 13 min read · Middle Way
When the Buddha woke up under the Bodhi tree and then gave his first teaching — the very first words of the dharma — he gave the Four Noble Truths. Not because they're the simplest thing. Because they're the most accurate map of the situation. That map is a donut.

The standard translation
is technically correct and
almost completely useless.

You have probably heard these: suffering exists, suffering has a cause, suffering can cease, there is a path to that cessation. True. All four statements are true. And for most people who hear them, they land with the impact of a fortune cookie. "Yes, suffering exists. Understood. What's next?"

The problem is that as discussed in the tradition — it is not actually possible for anyone except the highest-capacity beings to know what this means experientially through intellectual comprehension of that presentation. The words point at something. The something is what you need. And the gap between the words and the something is exactly the gap the path is designed to close.

So let me give you the bridge. Not my invention — just the version that transfers the view into your actual reality rather than leaving it as philosophy.

"Identity is an endless donut of self-stimulation and identitylessness is the donut hole. You cannot get out of the donut. You can only recognize the nature of your reality. Don't misunderstand."

— Future Buddha With Your Name, Yogi A-La

The Donut

The donut is not a metaphor I invented for accessibility. It's actually what's being described. Stay with it.

Samsara and nirvana are two a priori possibilities — two modes that exist before any particular experience. From inside samsara, you experience yourself as moving forward through time, through circumstances, through identities. You feel like you're getting somewhere. You might be moving very fast. But the structure of the experience is: you are moving inside the ring of the donut, going around the inside wall, and what you experience as forward motion is in fact circular. The donut keeps generating new scenery. The scenery is always more samsara.

The donut hole is not somewhere else. It's there — in the center of the donut you're already inside. You can't reach it by moving faster around the ring. You can't reach it by trying harder within the donut. The only thing that can happen is: you discover the inside wall of the donut hole. You feel it. And then, at some point, you understand the full extent of the implications — and you suddenly get hungry and eat the donut. Crazy that you could have done that the whole time.

That's the Four Noble Truths. Not as summary — as structure.

The Four, Correctly Understood

First Truth
Usually: "Suffering exists"

The pervasiveness of proliferating deluded experience

The full version: the pervasiveness of the proliferation of deluded experience, due to continuously following the path of engaging these proliferating appearances — appearances that are endowed with the possibility for further suffering — from the point of view of having incorrectly identified our situation in the first place.

Notice what's packed in here. It's not just "things are painful." It's that experience proliferates — generates more experience — on the basis of a fundamental misidentification. You took yourself to be something you're not, in a situation that is different from what it actually is, and everything that follows is generated by that original error. Suffering isn't an event. It's a mode of operation running continuously, generating more of itself, on the basis of original ignorance.

This is why you can have a good day and still be in samsara. The engine is running regardless of whether any particular moment is pleasant or unpleasant.

Second Truth
Usually: "Suffering has a cause"

The a priori possibility of samsara — and the donut

The cause of suffering is the a priori possibility of samsara itself, which is simply the absence of directly knowing the other a priori possibility. Each prevents you from experiencing the other, but each has a path for doing so. This is a donut.

The entire description of the donut includes the hole. This fact is called duality. The donut hole is dual with the donut. From the experiencer inside the donut, they experience a constant moving forward while standing in place. Or they discover the inside wall of the donut hole.

The cause of suffering isn't bad things happening to you. The cause is the structural situation of being inside the donut and not knowing it — not knowing there's a hole, not knowing there's another mode, not knowing that the a priori possibility of nirvana exists as actual, true reality even right now.

Third Truth
Usually: "Suffering can cease"

Awakening to the a priori possibility of nirvana

Awakening to the a priori possibility of nirvana as actual, true reality — something never recognized by sentient beings even though it could be. But when you realize the full extent of the implications of the inside wall of the donut hole, you suddenly get hungry and eat the donut. Crazy that you could have done that the whole time.

The cessation of suffering is not the end of experience. It's the end of the mode that generates suffering — the end of operating from original ignorance. Nirvana is not nothingness. It's the extinguishment of ignorance. What remains when ignorance is extinguished is not absence — it's clarity. The full, unobstructed capacity of the mind, no longer running the samsaric engine.

And this has been available the whole time. That's what makes it both liberating and slightly absurd.

Fourth Truth
Usually: "There is a path"

The abbreviated teachings that are the basis for the blooming of identityless wisdom

The path in its full expression is the basis for knowing — on the basis of direct experience — all phenomena of nirvana and samsara. Not knowing about them. Knowing them. The difference is the difference between reading about a smell and smelling it.

The word "abbreviated" here is important. All teachings are abbreviated because they can only point. They cannot deliver the experience directly. The experience must come from the inside — from what the tradition calls the Inner Teacher, which is your own Buddha nature meeting the teaching and recognizing something already present. The path creates the conditions for that recognition. It doesn't manufacture the thing being recognized. It was already there.

The Triple Gem:
the shade of the parasol

Once you understand the Four Truths, you understand why taking refuge makes sense. Going for refuge in the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha is not a religious ceremony you perform because the tradition says so. It's a recognition of where the reliable orientation actually is.

Some people describe this as going for refuge individually to each of the three, but that's a false separation — the Buddha and the Buddha's teachings are inseparable. It's easier to just understand that you are in the shade of the parasol. That's the image: a parasol extended over you, offering protection from the heat of samsara. You're either standing in the shade or you're not. Refuge is choosing to stand there.

On the word "Saha"

Why this world is called "World of No Fear"

The human world in Buddhist cosmology is called the Saha world. Saha means no fear — or more precisely, endurance, capability. Why call a world where everyone is basically afraid of everything "World of No Fear"? The commentaries say: because the sentient beings of this field endure desire, endure hatred, endure delusion, endure the fetters of conflicting emotion. They endure it all without fearing ignorance — which is to say, they endure all the symptoms while never addressing the cause.

On another level: those wishing for liberating wisdom must be fearless in the face of their own karma. The path requires that fearlessness. This is the Saha world — where those capable of fearlessness in the face of their own minds can discover the donut hole they've been circling all along.

The Four Noble Truths are not depressing. They are the most honest assessment of the situation available. And honesty about the situation is the only starting point for changing it. If you're inside the donut and don't know it, you'll keep running. If you're inside the donut and know it, you can start looking for the wall.

Every teaching that follows — every practice, every contemplation, every instruction — is in service of this: helping you find the wall, understand what the wall is, and eventually eat the donut. That's what the path is. It starts here.

Continue the Path

The engine that makes any of this work

Bodhichitta is not a warm-up. It's not preliminary sentiment. It's the only engine that turns personal practice into an actual path. Without it, meditation is just sitting. With it, everything becomes fuel.
