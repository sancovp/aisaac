Completely Fake Dharma

Why Most Meditation Instruction
Is Completely Fake

Isaac, Acharya · 12 min read · Shamatha & Vipassana
The mindfulness industry is worth $2.2 billion. That number tells you everything about what went wrong. What got extracted and sold was the container — the cushion, the app, the corporate wellness seminar. What got left behind was the thing that makes it work.

The problem isn't that people are meditating.
It's that they think they are.

Mindfulness as it's taught in the West is not fake because people are lying to you. It's fake because it was extracted from a context that gave it meaning and then repackaged without that context. What you're left with is a stress-reduction technique dressed in the language of liberation.

The technique itself is fine. Sitting still, watching the breath, noticing when your mind wanders — that's real. That's the first rung. The problem is when the first rung gets sold as the ladder.

When Dudjom Rinpoche — one of the greatest Dzogchen masters of the 20th century — gave meditation instruction, he always framed it in terms of View, Meditation, and Conduct. Not just Meditation. The View is what you're orienting toward. The Conduct is what you do with what you find. Meditation without View is just sitting. It has no vector. It goes nowhere.

"The point is to have Bodhichitta, develop shamatha and vipassana, and then realize the sambhogakaya through comprehending the basis and turning vipassana towards the mandala of awareness. It's that simple."

— Mahajala teachings

That's the whole path. Three sentences. Not particularly mysterious. But notice what's in those three sentences that isn't in your MBSR course: Bodhichitta, and a destination that isn't just "less stressed."

What Shamatha Actually Is

Shamatha means calm abiding. But the Tibetan tradition is very specific about what that means. It's not relaxation. It's not being peaceful. Relaxation is what happens when you stop being stressed. Shamatha is what happens when you train the mind to be stable — stable enough to hold an object, any object, without being dragged away by association, emotion, or restlessness.

The traditional texts describe nine stages of shamatha development. Most people who have been "meditating" for years haven't made it past stage one. They've been doing something that feels like meditation — there are moments of quiet, there's less reactivity, sleep improves — but the actual capacity being cultivated in the tradition, the capacity to sustain attention at will for four hours, hasn't developed.

This isn't a criticism of people who meditate. It's a description of what the instruction failed to convey. The tradition has very clear milestones. The Western adaptation threw out the milestones and kept the sitting.

Teaching

The ADHD misunderstanding

Here's something nobody in the mindfulness space will tell you: the wandering, distracted baseline state of the untrained mind isn't a disorder. It's just stage one. The texts are completely clear on this — there's no history of there ever being an obstacle that works like ADHD in the context of shamatha training, because what we now call ADHD is just the normal baseline before training. Stage one IS the monkey mind. It's where everyone starts. The instruction is: force yourself to stop for one second. That's the practice. Chain: stop, check posture, check the object, stop again. You're not broken. You're just at the beginning.

The Vipassana Problem

Vipassana is where the real failure of Western instruction shows up. Vipassana means insight. Specifically, insight into the nature of the mind itself — impermanence, the absence of a fixed self, the way phenomena arise and dissolve.

In the tradition, vipassana arises naturally from sustained shamatha. You can't force it. You stabilize attention first, then you open it. Then insight arises because you're looking clearly for the first time. It's not a technique you add. It's what happens when the mind is stable enough to see itself.

What the West did with vipassana is extract the investigation — the noting practice, the labeling, the body-scanning — and teach it as a primary technique divorced from the shamatha base that makes it work. So you get people doing body scans who have no attention stability, using a vipassana technique without vipassana's actual function, and then wondering why after five years of practice they still fall apart when something stressful happens.

Shamatha builds the container. Vipassana puts something in it. Without the container, you're just pouring water on sand.

"Progressively lift the forcefulness from the object induction → open placement shamatha → vipassana naturally → prajna. Then you are done. You are literally gaining supercognition."

— Mahajala teachings

The Missing Ingredient:
Bodhichitta

Here's the most important thing that got left behind, and also the one that sounds most like soft spiritual talk, so I'm going to be direct about it.

Bodhichitta — the mind of awakening, the wish to achieve liberation for the benefit of all beings — is not a preliminary warm-up. It's not a nice intention you set before your session. In the Mahayana and Vajrayana traditions, it is the actual engine of the path. Without it, what you're doing is personal development. With it, what you're doing is a vehicle.

The distinction matters because the path is not designed to make you a better version of yourself. It's designed to cut through the project of self-improvement entirely. Bodhichitta is what orients the path away from yourself and toward beings. That reorientation is what prevents meditation from becoming another ego project — which is exactly what happens when it's stripped out.

Look at the meditation teacher scene. People who have been practicing for decades and are still primarily concerned with their own states, their own realizations, their own credentials. The tradition has a diagnosis for this: it's the subtlest form of spiritual materialism, the accumulation of practice as possession. The antidote was always Bodhichitta, and it was always in the instructions. It just got edited out somewhere between Dharamsala and the wellness app.

What Actual Instruction Looks Like

The traditional instruction for shamatha-vipassana, stripped to its functional core, looks like this:

  • 1Generate Bodhichitta. Mean it. Even if you don't feel it yet, train the attitude. "I am doing this for the benefit of all beings." Not as performance — as direction of intent.
  • 2Take an object. The breath is fine. The point is that you commit to it. One object, consistently.
  • 3When the mind wanders — not if, when — return. Without drama, without self-criticism, without treating the wandering as failure. Return is the practice.
  • 4Build duration. Five minutes of real attention is more valuable than an hour of something that feels like meditation. Extend it when it's stable, not before.
  • 5Dedicate the merit. What you did, you give to beings. This is not symbolic. It's closing the loop on the Bodhichitta you opened with.

That's what a Mahayana Acharya would actually give someone as beginning practice. Not a watered-down summary of a tradition. The actual traditional instruction, as it's been given for centuries.

There's nothing exotic about it. It's what's been taught in the Kagyu and Nyingma traditions for centuries. The sophistication comes later — with Mahamudra, with Dzogchen, with the union of those two views that Milarepa and Longchenpa point toward from different angles. But you have to be able to sit first.

The Complete Path in One Paragraph

For anyone who wants the whole arc clearly stated: You are a mindstream. You have attention — place it until you can. Let insight arise. Now hold both — stabilized attention and natural insight — and you have prajna, wisdom. Don't interfere with prajna, let it self-liberate whatever arises. See that pattern of self-liberation repeating. Become confident in it. The boundary between samsara and nirvana clarifies. Intense visionary states arise and collapse. What remains is a normal person who is no longer confused about the nature of mind. That's the path. It takes as long as it takes and it starts with the breath.

The mindfulness industry isn't evil. It introduced a lot of people to something that points in the right direction. But pointing and arriving are different. If you've been pointing for years and wondering why you haven't moved, the answer is usually one of three things: the Bodhichitta isn't there, the shamatha base isn't built, or nobody told you about vipassana's actual function. Those are fixable.
