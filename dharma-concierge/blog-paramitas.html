Applied Bodhichitta

The Six
Paramitas

Yogi A-La, Acharya · 16 min read · The Six Perfections
The rubric for the application of Bodhichitta is the six paramitas — the six perfections. Actions you always undertake for the accomplishment of the twofold benefit: benefit of self and others simultaneously. This is what applied Bodhichitta actually looks like in a life.

Where does the eightfold path fit?
Inside the sixth paramita.

If you've studied Buddhist basics, you know the Noble Eightfold Path — right view, right intention, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. It's a complete ethical and contemplative framework for individual liberation, given in the First Turning of the Wheel.

In the Mahayana, that entire framework is subsumed within the sixth paramita, prajna — wisdom. This isn't a demotion. It's a recognition that the eightfold path, like every other method, finds its ground in wisdom, and wisdom in the Mahayana context is inseparable from Bodhichitta, the view of emptiness, and the orientation toward all beings. The container gets larger. Everything fits.

This is one expression of what might be called the holographic principle of the paramitas: each one contains all the others. Genuine generosity contains discipline, patience, diligence, meditation, and wisdom. Genuine wisdom contains the capacity for all the others. They are not separate items on a checklist. They are six facets of the same thing, each one a complete expression of Bodhichitta in action.

"The Six Paramitas are actions one should always undertake for the accomplishment of the twofold benefit — of self and others."

— Future Buddha With Your Name, Yogi A-La

The Six Perfections

First Paramita

Generosity — Dana

This seems straightforward until you actually look at what it means. The perfection of generosity is the mind actualized in its willingness to give anything circumstantially needed for the benefit of all — from pennies all the way up to the possibility of enlightenment itself.

Read that again: all the way up to the possibility of enlightenment itself. This is not "be a generous person." This is the recognition that the bodhisattva's ultimate gift is their own awakening — because through awakening, they become capable of benefiting beings in ways that no unawakened being can. The endpoint of generosity is the realization of identitylessness through giving one's entire mindstream to all via awakening.

On the way there: money, time, attention, protection from fear, the dharma itself — everything given according to what is circumstantially needed, without grasping, without the sense of a giver giving to a receiver. The hologram of all six paramitas is already present in genuine generosity: it requires discipline not to give what harms, patience to give to difficult people, diligence to keep giving, meditation to give from a stable mind, and wisdom to know what actually benefits.

Second Paramita

Discipline — Sila

Discipline here is not a list of rules to follow. It is never parting from the mind familiarity cultivated in meditation, and never faltering on one's ethical commitments — which means confessing openly whatever one believes one has done contrary to the teachings.

That second part is important and usually omitted. The discipline is not about never making mistakes. It's about the relationship with mistakes — open confession, without denial, without suppression, without the pride that refuses to acknowledge when the path has been abandoned. The bodhisattva who confesses openly and returns to the path is more disciplined than the practitioner who never admits error and therefore never actually corrects course.

The inner meaning: staying connected to the quality of mind — the open, clear, bodhichitta-oriented quality — regardless of circumstances. That is the thread. Discipline is not losing the thread.

Third Paramita

Patience — Kshanti

The mind actualized in its willingness to endure anything circumstantially — from discomfort to inconceivably excruciating psychic and physical pain — for the benefit of all.

Again: all the way to the limit. Not "be patient with annoying people." The perfection of patience is the capacity to remain open, remain oriented toward beings, remain on the path — regardless of what arises. Including what is conventionally considered unbearable.

The tradition distinguishes several types of patience: patience with harm, patience with suffering, and patience with the profound nature of reality — the patience to hold uncertainty, to hold paradox, to hold the recognition that things are not as they appear, without collapsing into either denial or despair. That third type — patience with the nature of reality — is where the other two ultimately lead.

Patience and bodhichitta are almost the same thing from different angles. You can endure anything when the orientation is genuinely toward beings rather than toward your own comfort.

Fourth Paramita

Diligence — Virya

To pursue what is positive and only what is positive.

Short. Precise. Everything that could be elaborated is already contained in that statement if you apply it seriously. What counts as positive? Anything that benefits beings. Anything that moves toward awakening. Anything that does not generate more suffering, more confusion, more of the same samsaric engine running.

The tradition is unambiguous: the bodhisattva does not pursue a mixed bag of positive and neutral and occasionally negative activities while maintaining good intentions. The perfection of diligence is not mixing things. It is single-pointed in the direction of benefit. This is where the willpower of the bodhisattva exceeds that of the ordinary person — not through the quantity of effort, but through the quality of orientation. There is no half-heartedness in it. Because the scope of the benefit being pursued is all beings for all time, anything less than total engagement is simply not proportionate to the task.

Fifth Paramita

Meditation — Dhyana

Gaining familiarity with the mind through right concentration, which leads to sharpening prajna.

This is the bridge. The first four paramitas — generosity, discipline, patience, diligence — are the ethical and motivational ground. The sixth paramita, prajna, is the wisdom that sees reality directly. Meditation is what connects them. It is the training that builds the container stable enough to hold what prajna reveals.

Right concentration here means shamatha — the calm abiding that allows the mind to stay with an object, hold an orientation, remain stable under the pressure of whatever arises. Without it, the first four paramitas lack the clarity to be genuinely effective. With it, they become precise. Prajna cannot arise in a turbulent mind. Meditation is what stills the turbulence.

The entire teaching on shamatha and vipassana from the previous post belongs here — inside this fifth paramita. It is the technical means by which the mind becomes capable of the sixth.

Sixth Paramita

Prajna — Wisdom

This one requires more space. Prajna is not information. It is not conceptual understanding of the teachings. It is the mind that has cultivated familiarity to its limit: the liberation of conceptual identity into identitylessness — not a "self" or a "no-self" but a Middle, true non-self. It has four distinct aspects, and they are not equal.

Aspect of Skillful Means

The common siddhis from cultivation of Bodhichitta: access to pure realms, increasing capacity to engage the six paramitas, control of emotional phenomena, emanations, the ability to possess each of the 84,000 dharmas that liberate from the 84,000 afflictive states. These arise as a natural consequence of genuine practice. They are not the point. They are what happens along the way.

Aspect of Strength

The superior willpower of a bodhisattva compared to an ordinary person. Due to this power, a true bodhisattva is like a superhero compared to a normal person regarding ability to benefit others — because it grants incredible mental stability, and through that stability neither the heroic motivation nor the concentration can be perturbed. The engine does not stall. The orientation does not flip under pressure.

Aspect of Aspirations

The aspect of wisdom from which perfect aspiration for relative methods — for the temporary and ultimate benefit of sentient beings — wells forth in increasingly clear expressions. The accumulation of perfect aspirations and their resultant activities is like Avalokitesvara's spontaneously responsive arms emanating as progress on the path. With the realization of jnana, this becomes the perfect expressive capability of the Greatly Able Sage: the ability to possess the modes of expression of the 84,000 dharmas for the 84,000 afflicted states of sentient beings throughout all world systems. The ability to lead them all.

Aspect of Jnana

The cultivation of mind familiarity's limit: the liberation of conceptual identity into identitylessness. This is the supreme siddhi that empowers all common siddhis and causes the ray-like bodhisattva emanations of the Buddha to actualize benefit throughout all nirmanakaya fields. This is where the path completes itself — not at a fixed endpoint but as a continuously expanding recognition of what was always present.

The holographic principle:
each one contains all others

The paramitas are not sequential stages you pass through and leave behind. They are a single integrated mode of operation, approached from six different angles. When any one of them is genuinely present, the others are necessarily present too.

You cannot have genuine wisdom without generosity — a mind that holds tightly to its own realizations, its own attainments, its own credentials, has not realized identitylessness. You cannot have genuine patience without discipline — the ability to endure is the ability to stay on the path. You cannot have genuine diligence without meditation — the mental stability to pursue only what is positive requires training, not just intention. And all of them, in every direction, point toward and require each other.

This is also why the tradition says the eightfold path is subsumed within prajna. Not because right speech and right livelihood and right mindfulness don't matter — they matter enormously — but because when wisdom is genuine, the other seven aspects of the eightfold path arise naturally from it. The framework gets larger. The container gets larger. Everything fits.

The complete arc

From the Four Thoughts to the Six Paramitas

The Four Thoughts turn the mind. The Four Noble Truths reveal the structure of the situation. Bodhichitta orients the path away from self-improvement and toward all beings. The six paramitas are what that orientation looks like in practice — what applied Bodhichitta actually means, day by day, in every circumstance.

This is the entire arc of the beginning of the Mahayana path. Not complicated. Not hidden. Not requiring special initiation to access. Requiring practice, requiring honesty, requiring the willingness to let these teachings actually land — not as philosophy but as a description of how to live. Everything more advanced — Mahamudra, Dzogchen, the vajrayana practices — is built on this foundation. If this foundation is not present, the more advanced teachings have nothing to land on.

Build the foundation. Mean it. The rest emerges.

The dedication of merit closes each session of practice in the tradition. Everything done — every moment of attention, every act of generosity, every instant of patience — is dedicated outward. Given to the awakening nexus of all beings. This is the loop closing on the Bodhichitta you opened the session with. The Sanskrit is: By this merit may all attain omniscience, and defeat the enemy, wrong-doing. From the turbulence of birth, old age, sickness, and death, from the ocean of samsara — may we all be free.

That dedication is not symbolic. It is the final act of applied Bodhichitta in every session. The merit of practice does not belong to the practitioner. It never did. It belongs to beings.
