Subtle Body & Sacred Medicine

Windhorse: What the Tibetans Know
About Energy That You Don't

Isaac, Acharya · 14 min read · Tsa / Lung / Tigle
Every spiritual tradition that actually works has a model of the subtle body. The West rediscovered this in the 1960s, called it "energy work," stripped out the technical framework, and has been selling incense and vibes ever since. What got left behind is one of the most precise and empirically grounded physiological maps ever constructed.

The word "energy" is doing a lot of heavy lifting

When someone in the West says "I felt a lot of energy in that room" or "my energy was low today," they're pointing at something real. There's a phenomenon happening. But the pointing is vague in the same way that saying "my insides hurt" is vague. It's true, and it tells you almost nothing about what to do.

The Tibetan system has been mapping this territory for over a thousand years. It is not metaphorical. It is not poetic. It is a technical account of how awareness, breath, and physical vitality interact — and what happens when those three are in alignment or out of it. The map has clinical precision. The West's version of energy work has vibes.

This matters because if you want to actually work with what the tradition calls energy — if you want to use it to deepen practice, strengthen vitality, or support the kind of intensive retreat work that produces real results — you need the framework. Otherwise you're trying to tune an engine with a general sense that something is mechanical.

The Three: Tsa, Lung, Tigle

The Tibetan subtle body system is organized around three primary terms. These are not invented categories — they describe functional realities that interact in specific, trainable ways:

Tsa
Channels / Nadis

The network of subtle channels through which energy moves. The primary channels are the central channel (uma), the right channel (roma), and the left channel (kyangma). Tens of thousands of subsidiary channels distribute throughout the body. Blockages in the channels produce suffering. Open channels produce vitality and clarity.

Lung
Wind / Prana / Vayu

The subtle wind energies that move through the channels. Five primary lung-energies govern specific physiological and mental functions: life-sustaining wind, upward-moving wind, pervading wind, fire-accompanying wind, and downward-clearing wind. The mind rides on lung — this is why breath practices stabilize the mind.

Tigle
Essence Drops / Bindu

The subtle essence drops, the quintessence of awareness concentrated in the body. The white tigle at the crown comes from the father's essence. The red tigle at the navel comes from the mother's essence. The indestructible tigle at the heart holds the very subtle wind-mind and is the seat of consciousness throughout lifetimes.

These three are inseparable in practice. You cannot work with one without affecting the others. Breath practices (pranayama, tummo, vase breathing) work primarily on lung. Physical practices (yantra yoga, trul khor) work primarily on tsa. The most advanced practices work directly with tigle — but only after the channels are open enough to support it.

Lungta — Windhorse

Lungta, often translated as windhorse, is one of the most important concepts in Tibetan culture and largely absent from Western dharma instruction. It refers to the vital energy of good fortune — the quality of energy that carries your activity, your aspiration, your life-force forward with momentum and effectiveness.

High lungta is what it feels like when everything is going right, when your activities succeed effortlessly, when obstacles dissolve and connections form spontaneously. It's not luck in the Western sense — it's a quality of energetic alignment that can be cultivated, strengthened, and protected.

Low lungta is what it feels like when everything is heavy. Projects don't move. Relationships are draining. Practice feels dry. The body is tired in ways sleep doesn't fix. In the Tibetan view, this isn't just a bad week — it's a sign that the vitality-current is depleted or blocked, and that specific practices are needed to restore it.

"The original text describes this chulen as a support for windhorse — the vital energy of practice."

— Dharma Concierge, on Ju Mipham's Black Manjushri Chulen

Windhorse practices are common in the Tibetan tradition: lungta offerings, smoke offerings, specific mantras, visualizations of the windhorse rising through the chakras. These are not superstitions. They are technologies for working with the very real phenomenon of vitality and its relationship to practice.

Chulen — Essence Extraction

This is where the tradition gets interesting in ways that modern practitioners are completely unprepared for.

Chulen literally means "taking the essence." It refers to a category of practice — and of material support — that works with the tigle dimension of the subtle body. The idea is that ordinary food nourishes the gross body, but the practice of chulen extracts and uses the subtle nutritive essence that sustains the energy body and, by extension, the depth of practice.

This isn't metaphorical. Ju Mipham Rinpoche — one of the greatest scholars and practitioners of the 19th century, whose Black Manjushri practice was his personal yidam throughout his entire life — left recipes. Specific formulations of herbs, mineral substances, and blessing-active materials that function as chulen in the traditional sense.

Chatral Rinpoche, one of the most revered Dzogchen masters of the 20th century, used chulen for the last 25 years of his life and lived to 102. The connection isn't incidental.

Technical Note

What "blessing-active" means

When the tradition describes a substance as blessing-active, it's not using vague language. It's describing a substance that interacts with the tigle and lung dimensions of the subtle body in ways that go beyond its gross chemical composition. This is why mantra recitation, visualization, and specific timing are part of the preparation — the gross material is a substrate for something more subtle.

Mipham's recipes are not folk medicine. They are formulated by someone who had complete knowledge of both the pharmacological and the subtle-body dimensions of the substances involved. The preparation follows strict tantric protocols. The result is something the tradition considers qualitatively different from even the same substances prepared without those protocols.

This is not a claim we expect you to accept on faith. The invitation is to use it and notice what happens to your practice. The tradition has extremely specific criteria for what "working" looks like: increased clarity in sitting, reduced gross hunger during intensive practice periods, more consistent access to the meditative states you're working with, and — over time — a perceptible strengthening of the vitality-current.

The Endogenous Map — What Neuroscience Is Starting to Find

The Tibetan model has a specific account of what happens in deep meditation at the level of the nervous system — an account that is startlingly consonant with what biochemistry is beginning to understand about endogenous altered states.

The "Kati channel" — a specific subtle channel described in dzogchen texts as running from the heart to the eyes — corresponds to what neuroscience now identifies as the visual cortex pathway for certain classes of endogenous experience. The tigle at the heart that "travels up to the eyes" in thogal practice is tracking something real about the physiology of visionary experience.

The tradition's account of what happens during deep practice — lung entering the central channel, the gross winds dissolving, the tigle at the heart becoming luminous — is a functional description, not a poetic one. It's describing a specific physiological event that occurs under specific conditions of practice. That event has measurable correlates.

What the West is doing with "energy work" is circling the edges of this without any of the navigational equipment. The Tibetan system gives you the map, the compass, and a thousand years of reports from people who made the journey. It also gives you the support materials: the chulen, the yantra, the pranayama practices, the dietary guidelines for intensive retreat. The whole thing is a system, not a collection of techniques.

Why This Matters for Your Daily Practice

You don't have to be doing advanced Vajrayana practice to benefit from understanding the subtle body. The basic framework changes how you relate to your practice in ordinary terms.

When practice feels dry, that's lung. The winds are disturbed — usually by too much mental activity, too much screen time, eating irregularly, not sleeping. The remedy is not to push harder in sitting. It's to work with the wind: slow the breath, regularize eating, get outside, reduce the cognitive load before you sit.

When you feel heavy and unmotivated, that's lungta. The vitality-current is depleted. The remedy is not more effort. It's lungta practice: smoke offering, mantra, physical movement, spending time in natural environments that restore rather than drain.

When practice suddenly opens and there's unusual clarity or visionary quality, that's tigle moving. Don't grasp it. Don't try to hold it. Just note that something is happening at a different level of the system than your usual thinking mind, and keep practicing.

The framework converts your practice from something you do to something you inhabit. You're not just sitting — you're working with a living system, attending to its actual state, giving it what it needs. That's the difference between a meditator who practices for years without much change and one who actually moves.
