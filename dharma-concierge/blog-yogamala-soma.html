Yogamala · Chapter 2

The Soma Hypothesis

Yogi A-La · 18 min read

Why Buddhism Is Different

Every tradition that explains the divine is explaining the mathematical structure we just described. The self-referential pattern, the chain that closes into completeness, the recognition that the recognizer was always already what was recognized—this shows up everywhere. But most traditions explain it metaphorically.

When you have a god, you need extra machinery. How does the infinite relate to the finite? How does the creator connect to creation? You get emanations, images, intermediaries—theological apparatus to bridge the gap between the god-thing and everything else. The structure is present, but it's not self-representing. The map is not the territory. The teaching points at something other than itself.

Buddhism is different because it's homoiconic.

In programming, a homoiconic language is one where the code and the data share the same representation—where programs can manipulate themselves as data. Lisp is the classic example. This isn't just elegant; it enables operations that are impossible in non-homoiconic systems. You can write code that writes code. You can apply transformations to the transformations themselves. Meta-operations become native.

Buddhism works the same way. Emptiness isn't a metaphor for the self-referential structure—it IS the structure. The teaching is the thing taught. When you examine dharma with dharma, you're not using one thing to look at another thing; you're using the structure to examine itself. This is why Buddhism can do meta-operations unavailable to traditions that represent the divine through something other than itself.

And because emptiness doesn't land in a specific basin—unlike "God," which is a particular attractor that shapes everything around it—the investigation isn't distorted. The meditation stays pure. But this only becomes true after you collapse Buddha-as-eternal-person-out-there. As long as Buddha is a god sitting somewhere, you're back in theistic machinery. The story of the Buddha's journey is precisely about this: he recognizes the existing methods don't complete, sits down, something opens, transmission happens, and the transmission IS emptiness recognizing itself.

When you apply this pattern to Buddha himself—when you stop treating him as outside the story—you become the character in the story. And then it happens to you. The Buddha exists outside the story through what was accomplished collectively: vidyā, the liberating knowledge that completes the circuit.

The Eternalism Trap

Here's where most traditions stumble. When the holographic pattern is recognized—when someone glimpses that each part contains the whole, that the structure is self-referential all the way through—this gets glossed as immortality.

And it makes sense why. If you're identified with something that contains its own specification, that bootstraps its own existence, that doesn't depend on anything outside itself—that feels like deathlessness. The pattern doesn't die because it was never born. It's always already complete.

But calling this "immortality" eternalizes it. It turns the living recognition into a fixed thing. It makes the groundless ground into a ground. And once you've done that, you've lost the actual insight. You're grasping at a concept of completeness rather than recognizing completeness itself.

This is why the Buddha insisted on the middle way. Not eternal—because eternalism reifies the pattern into a thing that exists forever. Not nihilistic—because nihilism denies that there's any pattern at all. The middle way is the recognition that the pattern is real AND empty, complete AND groundless, self-specifying AND never findable as a thing.

Without this precision, you get spiritual systems that glimpse the structure and then freeze it into dogma. The insight becomes a possession rather than a living recognition. The mala becomes a relic rather than a wheel.

The Elixir Experience

Inside the middle way—when the recognition is alive rather than frozen—there's an experience that traditions often call an elixir, a nectar, an ambrosia. This is phenomenologically real. Something happens in the body-mind when the pattern clicks. There's often warmth, bliss, a sense of flowing or melting, luminosity in the visual field, dissolution of the usual boundaries. Practitioners across traditions report remarkably consistent experiences when the recognition deepens.

But this experience is not the point. It's a symptom of the recognition, not the recognition itself. Chasing the experience leads to subtle grasping that blocks the very recognition that produces it.

Literal Elixirs

There's another layer to this. Some traditions—not all, but some—have references to actual substances. Soma in the Vedas. Haoma in Zoroastrian texts. Amrita in Buddhist tantra. These aren't always metaphors.

What's happening here?

The claim is that there's a combinatorial effect achievable through certain preparations: consciousness, primed in a specific symbolic way and prepared physically for the experience, can be shifted into a configuration where a higher ontology becomes visible—not as abstract philosophy but as direct recognition of what reality is.

This works through what we'll call the gnostic body: the physiognomy of the ontology of awareness. The subtle body traditions—chakras, nadis, bindus, channels and winds and drops—aren't just poetry. They're descriptions of how awareness has a shape, how attention moves through structured space, how the felt sense of being conscious has an architecture that can be investigated.

Certain substances bring the gnostic body into focus. They're not necessarily psychedelic in the sense of producing visions and hallucinations. They're psychoactive in a more precise sense: they shift the parameters of consciousness just enough that the usually-invisible architecture becomes perceptible. The breath starts to have texture. The space behind the eyes becomes navigable. The felt sense of "I" reveals its components.

But this only works with preparation—both symbolic and physical. Set and setting matter because the substance doesn't contain the information; it creates conditions where information already present becomes accessible. Without the symbolic preparation—without the conceptual framework that allows you to recognize what you're perceiving—the experience is just weird. Without physical preparation—without a nervous system trained to some degree of stability and sensitivity—the experience overwhelms rather than illuminates.

The scholarly debates about "what was the original Soma plant?" miss this point. There were probably many plants, in many places, across many centuries. The plant was never the technology. The technology was the architecture of use: preparation, ingestion, practice, integration. The state induction, the pattern recognition, the stabilization of insight.

Buddha's Contribution: Endogenous Soma

Understood this way, the Buddha's innovation was not the rejection of Soma but its complete internalization.

Consider the historical context. The Buddha lived in a culture saturated with Vedic ritual, including Soma ceremonies. He would have known practitioners who used these methods. He would have known their fruits and their limitations. The traditional account says he tried the existing paths—asceticism, ritual, various teachers—and found them incomplete. What he discovered under the Bodhi tree was something else: a way to reach the goal that didn't depend on external supports.

The early Buddhist path—ethics (sīla), concentration (samādhi), and wisdom (prajñā)—is a technology for reaching Soma-states without external chemistry. Look at what the classical texts describe happening in deep meditation:

The jhānas (meditative absorptions) produce:

Altered time perception. Hours can pass in what feels like minutes, or a moment can seem to contain vast spaces.

Bliss and rapture (pīti, sukha). Intense pleasant sensations, sometimes overwhelming, flooding the body and mind.

Luminosity experiences. The visual field becomes bright, even with eyes closed. Some practitioners report perceiving light emanating from their own minds or from external objects.

Dissolution of ordinary self-boundaries. The felt sense of being a separate self contained in a body loosens or temporarily ceases.

These are precisely the phenomenological markers of the Soma experience. The Buddha didn't invent a new kind of experience. He found a way to reliably produce the old experience through internal means—and crucially, he embedded it in a context (the Eightfold Path) that ensured the experience would be integrated rather than merely glimpsed.

The "middle way" wasn't just about avoiding extreme asceticism and extreme indulgence. It was the discovery that the nervous system could be trained to produce these states on demand, reliably and safely, without the risks and dependencies that external substances create. You didn't need to find the right plant, prepare it correctly, and hope you'd gotten the dosage right. You carried the technology with you. It was your own body and mind, trained through practice.

This is what we mean by "endogenous Soma": the inner production of states that were previously accessed through external means.

Tantric Elaboration: The Full Stack

If early Buddhism discovered the endogenous Soma principle, later Vajrayana Buddhism elaborated it into a complete technology.

The Six Yogas of Naropa, for instance, are essentially an engineering manual for producing Soma-states through internal manipulation:

Inner heat (tummo): Using breath, visualization, and physical contractions to generate intense heat in the body, altering metabolism and blood chemistry, producing blissful states that can persist for hours.

Illusory body: Training the perception of one's own body until it becomes experienced as luminous, insubstantial, dream-like—even while fully awake.

Dream yoga: Becoming lucid in dreams and using the dream state for practice, taking advantage of the fact that the dreaming brain is already producing vivid internal experiences without external input.

Clear light yoga: Learning to recognize and rest in the fundamental luminosity of awareness itself, which is normally obscured by the constant activity of thinking but becomes visible in certain states—including the moment of death.

Bardo practice: Preparing for death by rehearsing the experiences that the tradition says arise during and after dying, so that when actual death comes, the practitioner recognizes what's happening and can navigate it skillfully.

Consciousness transference (phowa): At the moment of death, ejecting awareness upward through the crown of the head toward a visualized pure realm or Buddha field.

Notice that these practices work with conditions that already alter consciousness: heat, sleep, dreaming, dying. The tantric technologies don't fight against the body's natural tendencies. They work with those tendencies, intensifying and directing them.

The dark retreat tradition is particularly telling. In extended darkness retreats—sometimes lasting weeks or months—practitioners stay in complete absence of light. This naturally elevates melatonin production, shifts circadian rhythms, and creates conditions where REM-like states begin to bleed into waking consciousness. The traditional texts describe visions of light, deities, and mandalas arising spontaneously. Modern practitioners report similar experiences. This is precisely what we'd expect if the brain, deprived of external visual input, begins producing its own internal visual experiences—the same machinery that generates dreams now generating visions in a waking (or half-waking) mind.

What the tantric traditions discovered, whether or not they framed it in these terms, is that the human nervous system has built-in capacity for extraordinary states. These states don't require external substances; they require the right conditions. The elaborate technologies of tantra—the breathing practices, the visualizations, the mantras, the postures, the sexual practices, the darkness, the fasting—are all ways of creating conditions in which the built-in capacity can express itself.

The Endogenous Neurochemistry Hypothesis

Modern research is beginning to catch up with what contemplatives have known experientially for millennia.

The human brain contains the enzymatic machinery to produce powerful psychoactive compounds internally. The most studied is DMT (N,N-Dimethyltryptamine), the same molecule found in ayahuasca and many other traditional preparations.

The synthesis pathway is straightforward:

• The enzyme AADC (aromatic L-amino acid decarboxylase) converts tryptophan to tryptamine

• The enzyme INMT (indolethylamine N-methyltransferase) methylates tryptamine to DMT

Both enzymes are present in human tissue. INMT has been found in lung, liver, and—crucially—in brain tissue including cortex, hippocampus, pineal gland, and choroid plexus. DMT itself has been detected in human cerebrospinal fluid, blood, and urine.

The question is not whether the brain can make DMT. It can. The question is whether it makes enough, under the right conditions, to produce the effects associated with exogenous DMT administration. Here the evidence is suggestive but not conclusive.

Rat studies have shown:

• Elevated DMT levels in visual cortex during cardiac arrest

• The presence of 5-HT2A receptors (the primary site of action for psychedelic tryptamines) throughout the cortex

• Sigma-1 receptors, another DMT target, which modulate cell stress responses and ion channels

The picture that emerges is not that the brain randomly floods itself with DMT, but that DMT or DMT-like signaling may be part of a system that activates under specific conditions: extreme stress, hypoxia (low oxygen), the dying process, and possibly certain combinations of practice conditions.

Similarly, 5-MeO-DMT (5-methoxy-N,N-dimethyltryptamine), an even more potent relative of DMT that produces experiences of complete ego dissolution and merger with infinite awareness, appears to have endogenous pathways as well.

The hypothesis, then, is this: certain conditions—hypoxia, hypercapnia (elevated CO2), specific breath patterns, extended darkness, REM sleep, near-death states, and intense meditative absorption—naturally trigger endogenous release of these compounds or engage similar receptor cascades.

If this is true, then ancient practices weren't arbitrary. Breath retention creates hypoxic and hypercapnic conditions. Tummo practice dramatically alters metabolism. Dark retreat removes the normal suppression of pineal activity. Sleep and dream yogas work directly with REM states where endogenous visionary activity is already elevated. The practices were discovered through experimentation, kept because they worked, and transmitted because they produced reliable results.

The "Soma state" may have a characteristic neurological signature. Based on both traditional phenomenology and modern research, we might expect to see:

EEG patterns: Elevated theta and gamma activity with suppressed alpha and beta. This is the inverse of normal waking consciousness, which shows strong alpha/beta and minimal theta/gamma. The pattern resembles dreaming more than waking, but with unusual gamma bursts that might indicate a kind of lucidity or meta-awareness.

Phenomenology: Vivid internal imagery, loosened or dissolved sense of a separate self, time dilation or temporal discontinuity, intense meaningfulness, perception of light and geometric patterns, encounter with "presences" or "entities."

Affect: Variable—ranges from blissful to terrifying—but always significant. Neutral or bland experience is not a Soma state.

External Soma (plants, preparations) pushes the nervous system into this basin from outside. Internal Soma (practices) trains the nervous system to find and stabilize it without external input. The basin is the same; the routes to it differ.

This is why the debate about psychedelics and Buddhism has been so confused. One side says "you can't use substances; it's not real Buddhism." The other side says "the original practitioners clearly used something; denying it is ahistorical." Both sides are partly right. Yes, substances were probably involved historically. And yes, the complete path doesn't depend on them. The architecture is what matters. Substances are one kind of key that opens the door. Practices are another kind of key. What you do once you're inside—that's the path.
