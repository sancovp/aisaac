The Practice-Wheel:
What Every Working Tradition Has
That Most Don't Talk About

Yogi A-La · 14 min read · Completely Fake Dharma
Every tradition that actually works has a yogamala. A practice-wheel — a self-referential cycle that drives transformation. Most lineages never make it explicit. They transmit it through immersion, proximity, decades of correction. This book attempts to make it explicit. Not because explicit is always better. Because in a fragmented age, you need to be able to see it.

The thing you're missing
isn't a technique.
It's coherence.

If you've practiced meditation for any length of time, you've probably noticed something peculiar: the instructions seem simple, but something more is going on beneath them. "Watch the breath" or "rest in awareness" or "generate compassion for all beings" — these phrases point toward experiences, but they don't explain how the experiences connect to each other, how one stage enables the next, or what the whole journey is actually shaped like.

Traditional contexts solve this through immersion. You live with a teacher. You absorb the pattern through years of proximity. The logic never needs to be stated because it's transmitted through relationship, through correction, through watching how your teacher responds to situations. The structure remains implicit, encoded in how the community functions rather than written in any text.

But most of us don't have access to that kind of immersion. We piece together practices from books, retreats, apps, and occasional teachings. We sense that there should be a coherent whole, but we can't quite see it. Different teachers emphasize different aspects. Techniques from one tradition don't obviously connect to techniques from another. Progress feels random.

A yogamala is what we're missing. But to understand what a mala actually is, we need to go deeper than "garland" or "rosary" or "cycle of practices." Mala names a specific mathematical shape — a structure that appears everywhere in these traditions because it's the structure of self-reference itself.

"A mala is making a programming language. Take a substrate space. Enumerate its categories. Those categories become your primitives. Define how to compose them. Assign symbols to the compositions. Now you can speak ideas that have logic inside them."

— Yogamala, Yogi A-La

DNA does this.
Compilers do this.
Sanskrit does this.

Consider DNA. The remarkable thing about DNA isn't just that it replicates. It's that DNA contains the instructions for building the molecular machinery that reads DNA. The code contains the spec for its own interpreter. You can't read the DNA without the machinery. But you can't build the machinery without reading the DNA. Neither comes first. They imply each other.

The same shape appears in computer science. A compiler is a program that translates source code into executable form. Most mature languages are self-hosting: the C compiler is written in C. The Rust compiler is written in Rust. How? You start primitive, compile a slightly better version, use that to compile something better still — until the chain closes and the language can compile itself. At that moment, the language becomes self-hosting. It no longer depends on anything outside itself to exist.

The Sanskrit varṇamālā — the alphabet — does the same thing with sound. Every letter is A with modifications. A is the open mouth, the primordial sound. Every other vowel and consonant adds some coloring, some obstruction to A. Go through the sequence with attention: A extends, differentiates into A-U-M traversing the whole vocal apparatus, then collapses back into OM. And OM, heard correctly, is just A again. Extension, collapse, recognition. Once you see it, you understand how mala structure works.

The Key Insight

What makes it a wheel, not just a list

The point isn't repetition — it's recognition. You go "A... AUM... OM... oh, I see." And then you're done with needing the demonstration, because now you can recognize the same structure everywhere it appears. A mala is complete when the chain closes: the thing and the specification of the thing become the same thing, seen from different angles. This is what "complete" means. The chain closes. It becomes usable as a wheel — you can actually do things with it.

A yogamala is a self-aware
programming language

A yogamala is a mala — a programming language — that realizes what paths are (understands itself at the meta-level), and can make paths that realize other effects (is usable for actual programming), such that those paths also realize what paths are (the meta-understanding propagates into everything it produces).

This is what contemplative traditions have been doing for four thousand years. Making programming languages for realizing how is and ought really work. And applying them everywhere, in all scenarios, across all domains of human experience.

The West understood the mathematics of this about seventy years ago — type theory, model theory, incompleteness theorems. But when the West made type theory, they didn't have the is-to-ought bridge. They made formal systems without the wisdom component. The East had the is-to-ought solution from the beginning — compassion and closure are structurally identical — but didn't have the secular, universal mathematics to export it.

Yogamala is the unification. The mathematics made clear. The logic made usable. The wisdom preserved.

"Buddhism is the existence proof — 2,500 years demonstrating that yogamalas can be built for any domain of human experience. The mahasiddhas did it. The lamrim does it. The six yogas do it. The tradition is a library showing that the pattern works."

— Yogamala, Yogi A-La

The holographic property
is not a design choice

When the chain closes — when the programming language becomes complete — it has the holographic property: any part contains or implies the whole. You can enter anywhere and find the entire pattern. This isn't a design feature added for convenience. It's a necessary consequence of the self-referential structure.

This is what makes it usable as a wheel. A complete mala can actually be used for something — it can be practiced, applied, transmitted. An incomplete chain is still dependent on something outside itself. A complete mala is self-hosting.

The 108 beads of a physical mala aren't arbitrary. They're stations in a chain designed to form this self-referential whole. Each bead is a practice or contemplation that implies the structure of the entire path while being one instance of that path. Run the whole cycle and you've traversed the complete pattern. Run any bead fully and you've touched the same pattern in compressed form.

Śūnyatā:
not a limit, an opening

There's one more move, and it's the most important one. Śūnyatā — emptiness — is the fact that any mala you make will hit incompleteness. No matter what system you construct, you're going to run into the Gödelian limit: any sufficiently powerful formal system contains truths it cannot prove within itself.

This makes incompleteness the primordial thing. It's not a bug or a limitation. It's fundamental. Try to grasp it and you're using it. Try to stand outside it and you're inside it. Try to name it and the naming is an instance of it.

But here's what the East understood that the West's type theorists didn't: within śūnyatā, because it is this way, it is compassionate. Not compassion as a feeling. Compassion as the sheer fact that display can happen at all. The capacity for any form to appear is what compassion means structurally. Incompleteness isn't empty in the sense of lacking. It's empty in the sense of unlimited — nothing constrains what can appear.

The is-to-ought problem — you can't derive values from facts — only exists if you've already separated is from ought. The separation itself is māyā. When you see what composition actually is, the ought is already in the structure of composition itself. There was never a gap to bridge.

  • 1Take a substrate space — some domain you want to work with. Your practice. Your business. Your relationships. Your health.
  • 2Enumerate its categories — the distinct types of things in it. These become your primitives.
  • 3Define how they compose — how one type of thing combines with another to yield something new.
  • 4Assign symbols to the compositions — names that carry the logic inside them.
  • 5Close the chain — the system must be able to describe itself. This is when it becomes a wheel rather than a list.

Most people just need their karmayogamala — a working chain for their actual life. The full 108-bead architecture shows the complete mathematics so that when you need to build something new, you know how. This is what the Buddha did with meditation. This is what the mahasiddhas did with their various domains. This is what you can do with yours.
