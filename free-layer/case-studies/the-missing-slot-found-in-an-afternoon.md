# Case study: the architecture that drifted for months, diagnosed in two minutes

**Source pattern (advanced):** SOSEEH + Mission Control — using a four-slot decomposition (forged via

then installing the simplest possible alignment check.

## The anecdote

An agent architecture had grown into something that couldn't be held in one head anymore. It kept
losing coherence — drifting from its mission, doing things nobody asked for, slowly becoming useless.
And the worst part: you couldn't even *talk* about the problem, because "it's drifting" produced only
generic advice. The thing that was wrong had no name.

The way out came sideways. Stuck, the mind reached for seemingly-unrelated content — Everest
expeditions, high-altitude reconnaissance flights, wingsuit jumps — not as procrastination, but
because those scenarios *felt like* the problem: one human doing something on top of a ridiculous
amount of invisible infrastructure. Stacking a few of those wildly different domains and asking "what
structure do these share?" collapsed the whole mess into four slots: something that steers, something
that carries, something that keeps it aligned, and the defined ways the pieces talk.

Applied back to the architecture, the diagnosis was *instant.* There was a steerer. There was a
vehicle. There were feedback loops. There was **nothing keeping it aligned** — nothing periodically
asking "is this activity still serving the goal?" That's why drift was inevitable. The empty slot was
the bug. And the fix turned out to be embarrassingly small: a short, honest, periodic check. Not a
yes/no rubber stamp — a forced statement of *the goal is X, current activity is Y, Y serves X because
Z.* If you can't say Z, you just caught the drift.

One person ran the same four-slot lens on their own agent setup and within ten minutes found three
people steering, nobody checking alignment, and ad-hoc handoffs. The chaos had been there for months.
The fix took an afternoon.

## What it reveals (the shape of the deeper rung)

- **The unlabeled problem becomes solvable the moment it has a vocabulary** — and you can *forge*
  that vocabulary from your own pre-conscious reaching plus cross-domain triangulation, rather than
  waiting for someone to name it for you.
- **The diagnosis is finite, not infinite.** "Everything feels broken" becomes four answerable
  questions, and the slot you can't answer cleanly *is* the failure. Almost always it's the
  alignment slot.
- **Completing the diagnosis grants vision.** Before the four-slot lens, the next steps were a fog of
  guesses; after it, the missing subsystem was *obvious* — not invented, revealed. That's what
  finishing a layer does: it hands you sight of the next one.
