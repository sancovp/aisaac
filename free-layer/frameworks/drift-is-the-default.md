# Drift Is The Default — correctness is a return, not a setup

**Altitude:** directional framework (a way to THINK). Hands you the class-shape, not the build.
**Invariant:** I7 (stochastic systems drift; staying on-target is an active repeated return) + I8
(observe to manage) + I6 (variability is energy).
**Derived from:** `mission-control.html`, `l6-concentration.html`, `concentration-engineering.html`,

## The shape

Any system that generates — an AI agent, a creative human mind, a working team — *drifts by
default.* Not because it's incapable, but because it's stochastic: it wanders off the thing it was
doing, a little each step, and twenty steps later it's confidently solving a different problem than
the one you gave it. Each individual step looked fine. The trajectory rotted.

The consequence: **correctness is not something you install once. It's something you maintain by
returning.** You externalize the goal, periodically check current activity against it, catch the
drift early, and steer back. The real performance number isn't "how good is each step" — it's *how
short the interval is between drifting and noticing.* This is exactly concentration in the
meditative sense: not the absence of wandering, but catching the wander sooner each time until the
return becomes continuous.

And the variability that causes drift is the *same* variability that produces novelty. You don't
want to eliminate it (that kills the engine) — you want to ride it, leaning into the drift early so
the trajectory stays on target.

## Why it matters

Long-running work fails in a way that's invisible until it's expensive. The session, the project,
the agent run all *look* productive the whole time — and then you look up and ask "what did we
actually accomplish?" Nobody made a dramatic mistake. There just was never anything checking, on a
short enough interval, whether all that motion still served the goal. The drift compounded silently.

This kills the most common false belief about AI (and about your own focus): that if you set it up
right, it'll stay on track. It won't. Nothing stays on track on its own. The teams, agents, and
people who *appear* to have superhuman focus are not drifting less — they're *returning* faster,
because something is checking alignment often enough to catch the wander while it's still small.

## How to think with it

1. Assume drift. For any extended piece of work, the question is not "will it drift?" but "how will
   I catch the drift early?"
2. Externalize the goal so it can be checked against. A goal that lives only in your head morphs and
   softens until there's nothing to compare current activity *to.*
3. Make the check *honest.* "Are we still on mission?" → "yep" → next, is theater. The real check
   forces specifics: the goal is X, current activity is Y, Y serves X *because Z*. If you can't say
   Z, you just caught the drift.
4. Shorten the interval. Check every ten minutes and you catch ten-minute drifts; check every four
   hours and you lose half a day. The frequency of the return *is* the quality of the work.

## What this framework deliberately does NOT give you (and where you go next)

This stops at the realization, on purpose. It hands you the law — drift is the default, and
staying on-target is a short-interval return maintained by an honest check — but not the machinery
that makes the return happen *without you*: the structures that fire the check on a schedule, that
detect drift from the work itself rather than from your willpower, that inject the correction so the
next step re-aligns invisibly, that let an unsupervised agent run for hours and stay on goal. That
gap is pacing, not a gate. First just *get* this: nothing stays on track on its own. Then go run an
honest check on your own work today and *experience the effect* — you'll catch a drift you'd have
missed. Keep shortening the interval and you *benefit*: the days that used to dissolve into busy
nothing start landing on the goal. Once returning-faster is a reflex, the next rung — building the
system that performs the return automatically, so multi-hour runs compound instead of degenerating —
is there for you to step into now, or to come meet the requirements for when you're ready.

Named plainly, because it's funnier true than hidden: this is itself an alignment check pointing you
at one direction. Just keep returning to it — keep thinking in drift-and-return and applying it — and
your own trajectory stays on the climb instead of wandering off it. I'm not gatekeeping the automatic
version; I'm making sure you've felt the manual return enough that the automatic one is a relief, not
a black box.
