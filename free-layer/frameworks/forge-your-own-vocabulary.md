## The shape

You can't discuss what you can't name, and you can't name what hasn't been collapsed into words yet.
A lot of the hardest problems aren't hard because they're complicated — they're hard because the
thing that's actually wrong *has no name yet,* so every attempt to talk about it (to a colleague, to
an AI) returns generic advice.

Two facts about how naming actually happens:

- **Your mind reaches before it speaks.** When you're stuck on a problem too big to hold, you feel
  pulled toward seemingly-unrelated content. That pull is not procrastination — it's your
  pre-conscious mind collecting *structural* analogies, scanning for domains that *feel like* your
  situation, before you have words for why. The discomfort is data.
- **The universal is in the intersection.** Stack several of those analogies *from deliberately
  different domains* and ask "what structure do these share?" — and the shared shape collapses out.
  The domain-variance is essential: same-domain examples only hand back what you already knew.

And once you *name* the shape, the name becomes **reusable equipment** — a two-minute lens you apply
to any future instance, and a shared metalanguage that quietly reshapes how you and any
collaborator (human or AI) process everything afterward.

## Why it matters

The default move when stuck is to push harder in the same vocabulary, or to go shopping for someone
else's framework. Both fail for genuinely novel problems, because the right framework *doesn't exist
yet* — nobody has named your specific failure pattern. Waiting for someone to name your problem for
you worked when change was slow. It doesn't anymore.

This reframes two things at once. First, the "distraction" you feel guilty about is often your best
pattern-recognition engine running; killing it cuts off insight at the source. Second, you don't
need to *find* the right framework — you can *forge* it, from your own pattern-matching, using an AI
as the thing that collapses the stack into a name. The payoff is asymmetric: one forging produces a
tool you reuse forever.

## How to think with it

1. When you're stuck and reaching, get curious instead of guilty: *what specifically* am I drawn to,
   and what do these things have in common? The catharsis is real — but the *selection* is signal.
2. Collect several analogies from genuinely different fields that *feel like* your problem. Resist
   picking examples from your own domain; that's the move that returns nothing new.
3. Triangulate: "what structure do these share?" The answer is the universal you couldn't name.
4. Validate by *click,* not proof — when the unlabeled thing suddenly has a name and you recognize
   it, that's the signal. If it doesn't click, your stack was too narrow; add more domain-variance.
5. Then *name and keep it.* The named shape is equipment now. You wear it; you don't re-derive it.

## What this framework deliberately does NOT give you (and where you go next)

This stops at the noticing, on purpose. It hands you the shape — your mind reaches for cross-domain
analogies, triangulating them collapses out a nameable universal, the name becomes durable equipment
— but not the operational craft of doing it *reliably and at depth*: how to structure the
triangulation so it produces a precise, typed, reusable shape rather than a vague metaphor; how to
install a forged framework so it conditions an AI's behavior turn after turn rather than being a
sentence it read once; how naming connects to a system that *accumulates* and composes the equipment
you forge. That withhold is pacing, not a wall. First just *get* this: your "distraction" while stuck
is raw material, and you can forge the name yourself. Then go forge one — stack a few wild analogies
and feel a nameless thing *click* into a word. That's *experiencing the effect*. Use the name a few
times and you *benefit*: a two-minute lens you keep forever. Once you've forged a few, the next rung —
a repeatable forge whose output compounds and conditions your AI — is yours to climb, now or after
you meet what it asks for.

Said straight, because the transparency is the point: I'm handing you simple categories — reach,
triangulate, name, keep — and pointing in a direction. Just keep thinking like this and keep
forging, and your own vocabulary compounds into equipment the next rung can be built from. I'm not
hoarding the forge; I'm making sure you've forged enough by hand that the automated forge is a
multiplier of something you already do, not a substitute for something you've never felt.
