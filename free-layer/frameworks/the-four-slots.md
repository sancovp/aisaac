# The Four Slots — how to see any system that has to survive complexity

**Altitude:** directional framework (a way to THINK). Hands you the class-shape, not the build.
**Invariant:** I3 (four roles + loops), supported by I2 (composition) and I8 (observe to manage).
**Derived from:** `soseeh.html`, `mission-control.html`, `interaction-loop.html`, `halo.html`,
`shell.html`.

---

## The shape

Anything that has to navigate complexity and not fall apart — a business, an AI agent, a team, a
life, an expedition — is the same four things plus the connections between them:

- **Something that steers.** The thing making the moment-to-moment decisions. (Who/what is the pilot?)
- **Something that carries.** The infrastructure that moves the work through the environment.
- **Something that keeps it aligned.** The part that periodically asks "are we still doing the
  thing we set out to do?" and corrects when the answer is no.
- **The defined ways the pieces talk.** The explicit protocol for how the parts hand off to each
  other — not "they can communicate" but "there are rules for how they communicate."

That's it. Four slots and the loops between them. Every system you've ever seen that works has all
four filled and connected; every system that's quietly broken has one of them missing or undefined.

## Why it matters

When a system *feels* chaotic — your business, your agent, your week — the instinct is to add more:
more tools, more people, more effort, more detail. That instinct is almost always wrong, because the
problem is rarely a missing *capability*. It's a missing *slot*. The most common missing slot, by
far, is the alignment one: nobody and nothing is checking whether all the busy activity still serves
the original goal. The system is productive and drifting at the same time.

The power of the four slots is that it turns "everything feels wrong, I don't know where to start"
into a finite diagnosis. You stop staring at a blob and start asking four answerable questions. And
the empty slot — the one where the answer feels weird or forced — **is the bug.** Not "might be."
*Is.* Every time.

## How to think with it

1. Take the thing that's overwhelming you. Don't try to fix it. Just try to *name* its four slots.
2. For each slot, ask the plain-language question: Who steers? What carries? What keeps it aligned?
   How do the pieces actually talk to each other?
3. The slot you can't answer cleanly is where the failure lives. The fix is to fill *that* slot, not
   to work harder on the slots that were already fine.
4. Notice that this composes upward and downward: the same four slots apply to your whole company,
   to one workflow inside it, and to a single collaboration session — same questions, different
   altitude.

## What this framework deliberately does NOT give you (and where you go next)

This stops at the diagnosis, on purpose. It hands you the four slots and the rule that the empty one
is the bug — but not the operational machinery for *enforcing* the slots once you've named them: the
mechanisms that make the alignment-check fire on a schedule, that make the handoff protocols binding
instead of advisory, that turn "we should check alignment" into a system that *cannot proceed
without* checking. That's deliberate, and it's not a wall — it's pacing. First just *get* this: name
four slots, find the empty one. Then go run it on the messiest thing you own and *experience the
effect* — the blob becomes a finite diagnosis. Keep doing it and you start *benefiting*: you stop
adding capability you didn't need and start filling the slot that was actually missing. Once that's
second nature, the next rung — building the structure that holds all four in place automatically,
under pressure — is there for you, and you can either step into it now or come meet the prerequisites
for it when you're ready.

And yes — naming what I'm doing: the layers build on each other, and this is the first one on purpose.
Just keep thinking in slots, keep applying it, and the climb keeps unfolding under your own feet. I'm
not gatekeeping the rung above; I'm making sure you've *lived* this one first, because that's the only
way the next one even makes sense.
